# 1) Recap: What is Arms Race Co-Evolution?
Arms race competition evolutions involve progress, mutual counter-adaptations between species.
- Species A will evolve to counteract species B, species B will then evolve to counteract species A, so on and so on.

Arms race competition evolution are continuous. They escalate over time.

Some examples of arms race competition evolutions are:
1) Predator-prey (cheetah gets faster to catch gazelles -> gazelles get faster to evade cheetahs)
2) Male-male competition.
3) Parasite-host (parasite becomes better at infecting ->  host evolves to have better immune system to fight parasite.)

# 2) Asymmetric vs Symmetric Arms Race
## Asymmetric Arms Race
An asymmetric arms race is defined as an "attack-defence arms race." The involved species have fundamentally opposite roles.
- One species is offensive.
- The other is defensive.

Asymmetric arms races result in different adaptations. This is because the traits evolving in the competing species are not the same.
- One species evolves traits to be offensive.
- Other species evolves traits to be defensive.

## Symmetric Arms Race
A symmetric arms race is where species evolve to improve the same trait or ability to do something.
- They compete in the same fitness landscape.

Key characteristics of symmetric arms races are...
1) They perform the same function.
	- Both species are doing the same thing. They compete to be better.
2) They adapt the same trait.
	- The same trait is being evolved.
3) Escalation dynamic.
	- Being slightly better than the average is what is important in symmetric arms races.

# 3) How do Arms Race Co-Evolution end?
Arms race co-evolution has 4 possible endings.
1) Extinction.
2) Unilateral Optimum.
3) Mutual optimum.
4) Cyclic.


## 1) Extinction
In asymmetric arms races, one species will drive the other to extinction.

In symmetric arms races, total extinction will occur.


## 2) Unilateral Optimum
In asymmetric arms races, one species evolves to find an optimum in the fitness landscape.
- Prevents the inferior species from evolving.

## 3) Mutual Optimum
The species both settle into an optimum in the fitness landscape with respects to their fitness functions.

## 4) Cyclic
Either species never settles into an optimum in the fitness landscape. One species will dominate in fitness whilst the other is dominated.

Over generations, the dominated species will evolve to become better that the dominant species, causing the dominant species to become dominated.
- Species oscillate in their fitness (Species A dominates species B -> species B dominates species A -> to infinity)

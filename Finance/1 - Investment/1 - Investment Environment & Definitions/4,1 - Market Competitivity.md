## Market Competitivity
Markets are highly competitive. Thousands of analysts and automated programs scour securities markets looking for the best buys to add to a portfolio.
- Expect to find very, very few opportunities to find securities which are obvious bargains.
- This is referred to as the "no free rides" rule.

## Risk-Return Trade-Off
Investments are made with the expectation of future returns. However, these returns are very rarely predicted precisely.

Risk will always be associated with any type of investment.
- Actual or realised returns from an investment will always probably deviate from the expected return anticipated at the start of an investment.

Assuming there is no risk, investors would be attracted to investments with the highest expected return.
- However, because of the "no free rides" rule, nothing comes without a cost. 
- If higher returns are desired, you expose yourself to extra risk in accepting a higher investment risk.

If investors expect a higher return from an investment can be made without bearing extra risk, investors will rush to buy that stock.
- This increases the price / share of the stock.

Individuals now considering to invest into the same share, which now has a higher cost, will find the investment less attractive.
- They perceive there to be less return expected from their initial investment amount.
- If you buy in at a higher price, your expected rate of return (profit per unit invested) is expected to be lower.

The asset being invested into is still viewed as attractive until some point in time, t, where it's expected return is equivalent, or in proportion, with the amount of risk taken.
- Investors, at this point in time, t, can expect a fair return relative to the asset's risk.

If assets returns were independent of risk, investors would rush to sell high-risk assets.
- Prices fall.
- Expected future rate of returns increase.
- Eventually becomes attractive enough to be once more included into a portfolio.


CONCLUSION: there is a risk-return trade-off in securities market.
- Higher-risk assets priced to offer higher expected return than lower risk assets.
- Risk should be managed and attempted to be measured [[4,2 - Manging and Measuring Risk]]

## Treasury Bills
US treasury bills are the most marketable of money market securities.

 US treasury bills, US-T bills, represent most simple form of borrowing.
 - US govt. raises money by selling US-T bills to the public.
 - Investors buy the bills at a discount from the stated maturity value.
 - At the bill's maturity point in time...
	 1) The holder of the US-T bill receives, from the government, a payment equal to the purchase price of the US-T bill.
	 2) The holder of the US-T bill also receives the difference between the US-T bill's purchase price and the ultimate value of the US-T bill at the point of maturity.
		 - This constitutes the earnings of the investor from the US-T bill.

US-T bills are issued with initial maturities of...
- 4 weeks
- 13 weeks
- 26 weeks
- 25 weeks

US-T bills can be purchased directly at auction OR on the secondary market from a government securities dealer.

US-T bills are highly liquid.
- Can easily be converted into cash and sold at a low transaction cost without much price risk.
- Unlike most other money market securities, US-T bills sell in a minimum of 100 dollars (10,000 dollars more common).

Bank Bond-Discount Method
Instead of providing prices of each bill, financial presses report yields based on those prices.

Yields are reported based on both bid and ask prices.
- Ask price is the price you would have to pay to buy a US-T bill from a securities dealer.
	- The price a seller is willing to accept for a bond.

- Bid price is the slightly lower price you would receive if you would want to sell a US-T bill to a dealer.
	- The price a buyer is willing to pay for a bond.
	
- Yield is the return the investor earns from the bond based on it's price

The bid-ask spread is the difference between the bid and ask price, which is the dealer's source of profit.

Bid yield is higher than the ask yield. This is because prices and yields are inversely related.
- When price goes up, yield goes down. Vice versa is true.
- Because bond is a fixed-income security. It's return is determined by a mathematical formula and is fixed.
- The more you pay, the less yield you get.


## Calculations associated with Bank-Discount Method
Using the bid and asked entries, you can calculate...
- The yield a dealer was willing to sell a bill for ($yield_{buy}$).
- The yield a dealer was willing to buy a bill for ($yield_{sell}$).

$$
yield_{sell} = yield_{asked} \cdot \frac{maturity}{360}
$$
$$
yield_{buy} = yield_{bid} \cdot \frac{maturity}{360}
$$
Using these percentages, the prices a...
- Dealer would sell a US-T bill for.
- Dealer would buy a US-T bill for.
... can be calculated

$$
price_{buy} = price_{bill} \cdot (1 - yield_{buy})
$$
$$
price_{sell} = price_{bill} \cdot (1-yield_{sell})
$$

An investor who buys a bill for a maturity m will see their investment grow over the m days by a multiple of $\frac{price_{bill}}{price_{sell}}$
- $1 - \frac{price_{bill}}{price_{sell}}$ = gain
- Multiplying the gain to a yearly rate with respect to maturity enables the ask yield to be computed.
## Weaknesses of Bank-Discount Method
Bank-discount method has a long tradition for computing yields. However, it has 2 issues.

1) Assumes that a year has only 360 days.
2) Computes the yield as a fraction of par value rather than the price the investor paid to acquire the US-T bill.

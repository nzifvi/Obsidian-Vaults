# Exerting Metacognitive Control
Research question: can people exert control over their decision biases

Research question tested with an experiment:
![[Pasted image 20260429134557.png]]
- People were shown a blank screen for $[0.5, 1.5]$ seconds.
- Then, for $0.5s$, a grid of lines were shown.
	- Some lines were orientated horizontally.
	- Other lines were orientated vertically.
- Participants asked to report whether lines were vertically or horizontally orientated.

People have a large bias towards reporting horizontal orientation in general.
- Does not mean performance is bad.
- Means that, if all other factors are equal (such as the number of horizontally and vertically orientated lines), people will be more likely to report all lines as horizontally orientated.

Three possible outcomes to experiment exist
1) Manipulation has no effect on performance (dprime: d') or bias (criterion)
2) Participants can reduce their bias whilst maintaining performance (d')
3) Participants reduce their bias by using a trivial response strat. which makes them worse at the task.

Research findings:
- Specific components of decision-making can be targeted: exerting metacognitive control over our bias.
	- Confidence-related brain activity could be controlled very well with little training.
	- In follow up tests, training high/low confidence brain activity 
- Collected brain activity during motion discrimination task.
- Trained a sparse logistic regression model to predict confidence ratings from patterns of collected brain activity in frontal regions.

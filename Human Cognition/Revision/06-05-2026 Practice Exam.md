## b)
Making group decisions can be difficult due to the fact that each individual, within said group, can arrive to different conclusions and be biased towards integrating different information into their individual decisions.

One model which can integrate the decisions of two individuals is Bayes optimal integration. By modelling an individual's decision as the two Gaussian distributions involved, you can produce a combined Gaussian distribution which acts as a reliability metric: indicating which decision of the two individuals is more reliable.

## c)
Driving is a task which requires the achievement of multiple sub-tasks simultaneously. 

Firstly, a driver must modulate the velocity of the car over time dependent on visual sensory information such as the distance to the car in front. Such a problem can be handled via predictive coding in which the brain is fed visual data, the brain makes predictions, and feeds down errors to control the modulation. 


Secondly, a driver must monitor their surroundings for hazards using visual sensory information. This requires the use of endogenous attention.

Thirdly, a driver must be able to modulate their endogenous attention to pay attention to multiple metrics such as the car's velocity, fuel, and or signs on the road. 

Fourthly, drivers must be able to navigate themselves towards their intended destination. This navigation is achieved via decisions on which roads to take. Decisions such as what is the right road to turn onto are confidence judgements. These confidence judgements are modelled using drift diffusion models which accumulate visual sensory information until either decision X (turn onto that road) or decision Y (remain on current road) is made.

Fifthly, drivers must be able to make predictions as to their position at t(i+1) using their current position and velocity to inform. This is yet another predictive coding decision.
## d)
One example of how the study of cognition has inspired AI systems is the case of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). 

CNNs mimic the hierarchical structure of the primary visual cortex used by humans to extract features acquired from neuron signals sourced from the eyes. In the case of a CNN, an image is fed into the input neurons within the input layer: mimicking how light waves are translated into electrical signals to be interpreted by the brain.

The CNN, as the inference progresses layer by layer, extracts increasingly complex features just as how the primary visual cortex does. In addition to this, the CNN's receptive field also increases as the layers are traversed during inference. Similarly, the primary visual cortex's receptive field also increases as the hierarchy is traversed upwards.

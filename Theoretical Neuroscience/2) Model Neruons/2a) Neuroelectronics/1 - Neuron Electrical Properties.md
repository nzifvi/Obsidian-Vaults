# Overview
Neurons are packed with a huge and varied number of ions and molecules.
- Many molecules carry charges (either positive of negative)
- Most of time, there is an excess concentration of negative charge inside a neuron.

The potential of extracellular fluid, outside of neuron, is defined as 0.

When a neuron is inactive, excess internal negative charges causes potential inside cell membrane to be negative.
- This negative potential = a point fo equilibrium where flow of ions in matches flow of ions out of cell.

Membrane potential can be changed if the ion flow is modified, breaking equilibrium, by opening or closing ion channels.

Under normal conditions, membrane potential varies from $[-90mV, 50mV]$

# Intracellular Resistance
Membrane potentials, measured at different places in a neuron, can have different values.
- Membrane potentials in soma, dendrite, and axon can all be different.

Potential differences between parts of neuron cause ions to flow within cell: normally equalising the potential differences.

Intracellular medium forms intracellular resistance.
- Intracellular resistances resists the equalising flow.

Intracellular resistance is highest for long, narrow stenches of dendrites or axon branches.

# Membrane Capacitance and Resistance
Intracellular resistance to current flow can cause large differences in membrane potential measured in different parts of neuron.

Cell membrane of neuron forms a capacitance $C_m$ known as the membrane capacitance.
- Caused due to excess negative charge on inside and balancing charge on the outer surface of neuron membrane.

The membrane potential $V$, and excess charge $Q$, are related by the membrane capacitance $C_m$
$$
Q=C_m V
$$
- Membrane capacitance is proportional to the total amount of membrane (surface area of membrane)
- Larger surface area -> larger membrane capacitance -> larger charge Q.
